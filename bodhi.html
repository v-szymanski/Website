REVVITY INTERNSHIP

Envisioning the Future of Plate Reader Applications for Clinical & Research Labs

Overview

The purpose of this project is to improve the experience of plate reader applications used in life sciences and diagnostics. Current products on the market are known to have poor usability with outdated and inconsistent interfaces, along with constant information overload. The goal is to create a flexible and scalable product that can work for any plate reader protocol. Through quick rounds of iteration we worked to design an MVP, guided by research insights from myself and other team members. I completed a persona study for this project, along with designs ranging from sketches to high-fidelity screens.

Background

Plate reader applications are typically used in clinical and laboratory settings, in which a technician needs to run a protocol or assay. Their workflow involves pipetting samples and reagents into well plate(s) (also known as micro-plates), setting up the hardware and software, and running the experiment. Current products on the market are known to have poor usability, outdated & inconsistent interfaces, and information overload.

There is a massive variety of assays that need to be accounted for when designing a general use plate reader. Currently, this range of use cases is covered by hundreds of existing products made by hundreds of companies. Since each product is built for a specific situation, they are relatively inflexible and the customer has to buy additional products if theyâ€™d like to expand their workflow.

The goal: Create a functional, flexible, usable, and delightful product that can essentially replace the majority of existing plate readers and fill this gap in the market.

User Research

Prior Research

The user research team provided us with a workflow analysis, mapping out the steps of a typical user setting up and running an experiment using a well plate. For the purpose of quickly reaching an MVP, we focused only on the simplest workflow, in which the user is selecting a pre-defined protocol. There are five main sections in the flow: sign in, select protocol, run protocol, view results, and export results. The product manager also provided us with a set of proto-personas to get started.

Competitive Analysis

Before diving into design, I worked with my co-designer on a competitive analysis to better understand the market. We looked at four existing products in the problem space and pulled out key insights from each one, highlighting what was unique along with the common themes across products. We also roughly mapped the workflow for each application to see which patterns are most common, as this may influence user expectations.

Design Process

Using the workflow analysis, proto-personas, and competitive analysis, we got started with the design process. When working in Figma, I used our design system patterns and components to achieve consistency with the rest of our brand. Check out revvity.design for more information.

Brainstorming & Sketching

I find it helpful to formulate my thoughts and ideas on paper first, so I started with a few (messy) sketches. Looking at references from our design system, the company's other products, and competitors, I began to plan out what the screens may look like. The most difficult part to plan was the navigation and overall structure, as this affected everything within it and determined the overall workflow. Since the project scope is so broad, it was challenging to narrow all the possibilities down into one navigation bar with clear steps. I used a compilation of products (from within and outside of the company) to inform my design options at this stage, along with the workflow map to see how the steps are typically divided.

I discussed the sketches with my co-designer and compared our ideas. We then moved into Figma and began rough layouts and wireframes, slowly building up the level of detail and making sure to leave notes on any questions or assumptions for future reference.

Decision Making Process

After the first round of iterating, we had multiple options for aspects concerning the layout and structure. One example was the layout for the primary call-to-action or next step indicator on each page. We deliberated over the best location to put this action, because depending on what screen was being shown, there were multiple options that could've worked, and there was not one easy answer that worked best for every screen. However, we decided on Layout 1 in the end, since having the CTA in the top right is a common pattern and it maximized vertical space while ensuring the action was always visible, even if the user hid the sidebar.

Final Designs

We iterated in each round and continued to add more features, eventually ending up with 10 final screens that illustrated the most critical aspects of the workflow. In this MVP, users can view and select their protocols from a card or table view. They can access a sidebar for making quick edits to the protocol/plate, or access more advanced settings to modify it further. They can also upload protocols from a file or create new ones. After selecting a protocol, they can confirm/edit the plate map, and are guided by the software at this stage to set up for the experiment. Once setup is complete, the experiment will run and display live updates on the screen. Finally, the user can access a variety of export settings to suit their needs.

Insights

This design project was very fast-paced, including three design reviews that took place only a few days apart, due to the tight timeline. I was surprised and proud to see how much we could accomplish in this timeframe, and helped me understand the value of sprints like this and the agile environment in which this took place.

Most existing applications in this field have crowded interfaces which can be overwhelming. Through progressive disclosure techniques, we can show the user exactly what they need for the task theyâ€™re performing, while keeping other information off the screen until they need it.

When working with complex and scientific applications, it's crucial to consult industry experts and/or proxy users throughout the process. They can help you block out the noise (regarding techincal jargon, understanding the science behind the process, etc.) and understand what users value.

Conclusions

We took all the functionality from multiple products to build a powerful, flexible, and scalable product with clear workflows and refreshed UI. Further rounds of iteration will be required to bring the project to an MVP stage, as we only had a few weeks to work on the project. However, the team learned a lot throughout this short sprint, and it was very impactful for me to work on the end-to-end experience of a complex application like this one.
